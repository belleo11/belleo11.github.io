Creatine — The Body's Own Energy Reserve

📅 February 2, 2025 🧪 Biochemistry 6 min read

Creatine is one of the most researched molecules in sports science. It plays a powerful role in cellular metabolism by enhancing energy generation in the body. While it is commonly used as a supplement to boost athletic performance, it's roles in disease prevention and mental health are only just beginning to be recognized.

Formula: C₄H₉N₃O₂
MW: 131.13 g/mol
Solubility: Slightly soluble in water
pH: Neutral
Precursors: Arginine, Glycine, Methionine
IUPAC Name: 2-(carbamimidoyl-methylamino)acetic acid
About half of the body's daily creatine requirement is produced endogenously, while the remainder is obtained through the diet, primarily from red meat and fish [4].

Energy Generation

The core function of creatine in the body is acting as a rapid energy buffer. It does this through its metabolised form: phosphocreatine (PCr).

When cells need energy fast — during intense exercise, for example — ATP (adenosine triphosphate) is consumed quickly. Phosphocreatine donates its phosphate group to ADP to regenerate ATP almost instantaneously, buying the cell time before slower metabolic pathways take over.

PCr + ADP + H⁺ ⟶ Creatine + ATP (catalysed by creatine kinase)

This reaction is reversible — during rest, excess ATP is used to convert creatine back into phosphocreatine, storing energy for the next burst of activity.

Where is it stored?

Approximately 95% of the body's creatine pool is stored in skeletal muscle, with the remaining 5% found in the brain, liver, and kidneys. Of the total creatine in muscle, roughly 60–70% exists as phosphocreatine at any given time [4].

Brain Function

The brain is an energetically exhausting organ — it accounts for roughly 20% of the body's total energy expenditure despite being only ~2% of body weight. Creatine plays a significant role in maintaining this energy supply, particularly in neurons during high cognitive demand.

Emerging research has linked creatine supplementation to improvements in mood and depressive symptoms. A 2012 study found measurable effects of creatine on brain energy metabolism and its potential as an adjunct treatment for depression [2].

Vegetarians and vegans tend to have lower baseline creatine levels since dietary creatine comes almost exclusively from meat and fish — making them particularly responsive to creatine supplementation in cognitive studies.

Muscle Growth

Beyond energy buffering, creatine has been shown to support muscle hypertrophy (growth) through several mechanisms:

  • Increased capacity for high-intensity training volume due to faster ATP regeneration
  • Cell volumisation — creatine draws water into muscle cells, increasing cell volume and potentially triggering anabolic signalling
  • Possible direct effects on protein synthesis pathways, though research is ongoing

Biochemistry — The Creatine Cycle

Synthesis

Creatine biosynthesis is a two-step process occurring across two organs:

  1. Kidney: Arginine and glycine combine to form guanidinoacetate (GAA)
  2. Liver: GAA is methylated by methionine (via SAM) to produce creatine

Creatine is then transported through the bloodstream and taken up by target tissues via a dedicated creatine transporter (CRT/SLC6A8).

Degradation

Both creatine and phosphocreatine spontaneously and non-enzymatically cyclise to form creatinine, which is excreted in urine. This is why creatinine is used as a clinical marker of kidney function — its excretion rate is relatively constant and reflects total muscle mass.

Beyond Muscle — Emerging Research

Recent research has revealed creatine's involvement in a surprisingly wide range of tissues and processes, including:

  • Immune function: Creatine kinase activity in macrophages links energy metabolism to immune signalling pathways
  • Cancer biology: Tumour cells may exploit creatine kinase (CKB) to gain energetic advantages — an active area of oncology research
  • Bone metabolism: Creatine may support osteoblast function
  • Photoreceptors: The retina has high energy demands and utilises the phosphocreatine system for visual function
  • Thermogenesis: Brown adipose tissue uses phosphocreatine cycling as a heat-generating mechanism
