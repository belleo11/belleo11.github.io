Glycerin — The Humble Molecule Behind Moisture

📅 February 6, 2025 🧪 Organic Chemistry 5 min read

Glycerin — also called glycerol — is one of those molecules that quietly shows up everywhere: in your moisturiser, your food, your medicine cabinet, and even in your body's own fat metabolism. Despite its simplicity, its chemistry is remarkably versatile.

Formula: C₃H₈O₃ MW: 92.09 g/mol IUPAC Name: 1,2,3-propanetriol Appearance: Clear, colourless, viscous liquid Taste: Sweet Solubility: Miscible with water in all proportions
Glycerol is a triol — it has three hydroxyl (–OH) groups, one on each carbon of its three-carbon backbone. This is what makes it so extraordinarily good at interacting with water molecules.

The Chemistry of Hydration

Glycerin's defining property is that it is hygroscopic — it actively attracts and holds water molecules. This happens because each of its three –OH groups can form hydrogen bonds with surrounding water, creating a stable hydration shell around the molecule.

In practical terms, this means glycerin doesn't just sit passively on the skin — it draws water toward itself, either from the deeper layers of the skin (the dermis) or from moisture in the surrounding atmosphere.

Humectant vs. Emollient vs. Occlusant

In skincare formulation, moisturising ingredients are categorised by their mechanism. Glycerin is a classic humectant:

  • Humectant (glycerin): Draws water into the skin
  • Emollient (oils, fatty acids): Smooths and softens by filling gaps between skin cells
  • Occlusant (petrolatum, waxes): Forms a physical barrier to prevent water loss

Glycerin is most effective when combined with an occlusant — without one, it can actually draw moisture out of the skin in very dry environments if atmospheric humidity is low [1].

Skincare Applications

Glycerin is one of the most widely used ingredients in cosmetic formulation, appearing in moisturisers, serums, cleansers, and toners. Its popularity stems from several properties:

  • Non-comedogenic (doesn't clog pores)
  • Compatible with almost all other ingredients
  • Effective across a wide range of concentrations
  • Supports the skin barrier by reinforcing natural moisturising factors (NMFs)
  • Has demonstrated wound-healing properties in higher concentrations
At concentrations above 30%, glycerin becomes antimicrobial — it creates an osmotic environment hostile to bacteria. This is exploited in some wound-care products.

Food Industry

Glycerin (labelled as E422 in the EU) is used as a food additive for several purposes:

  • Humectant: Keeps baked goods soft and moist by retaining water
  • Sweetener: It has about 60% the sweetness of sucrose with a lower glycaemic index
  • Solvent: Used to dissolve flavours and food colourings
  • Thickener: Contributes to texture in confectionery and liqueurs

Pharmaceutical Uses

Laxatives

Glycerin suppositories are a well-established treatment for constipation. They work through two mechanisms:

  1. Osmotic effect: Glycerin draws water into the intestinal lumen, softening stool and increasing bulk
  2. Local irritation: Mild stimulation of rectal mucosa triggers peristaltic contractions

Eye Drops

Glycerin is used in ophthalmic preparations as a lubricant and moisture-retaining agent for dry eye syndrome. Its viscosity helps it remain on the ocular surface longer than water-based drops, providing prolonged relief.

Hyperhydration in Athletics

One of the more unusual applications of glycerin is in sports science. Glycerin-induced hyperhydration refers to the practice of ingesting glycerin with large amounts of water before exercise to expand total body water beyond normal levels.

Because glycerin is retained in the body's fluid compartments (rather than being quickly excreted like excess water), it creates an osmotic gradient that encourages the body to hold onto more water than usual. Research has shown this can improve endurance performance and thermoregulation in hot conditions by delaying dehydration [2].

Glycerol was actually banned by WADA (the World Anti-Doping Agency) from 2010–2018 as a masking agent, before being removed from the prohibited list when further evidence deemed it not performance-enhancing enough to warrant restriction.

Where Does Glycerin Come From?

Industrially, most glycerin is a byproduct of biodiesel production. When vegetable oils or animal fats are transesterified to make biodiesel, glycerol is released as a coproduct. The massive growth of the biodiesel industry has actually created a global glycerin surplus in recent decades.

It also occurs naturally in the body — glycerol forms the backbone of triglycerides (dietary fats) and phospholipids. When fats are broken down via lipolysis, glycerol is released and can be used as a substrate for gluconeogenesis (the synthesis of glucose).
