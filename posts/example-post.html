Capsaicin — The Chemistry of Heat

📅 April 7, 2026 🧪 Organic 5 min read

If you've ever bitten into a chilli pepper and felt that immediate, burning sensation, you've experienced capsaicin firsthand. But what exactly is happening at a molecular level?

What is Capsaicin?

Capsaicin is a naturally occurring alkaloid found in chilli peppers of the genus Capsicum. It's classified as a vanilloid compound due to its structural similarity to vanillin (the primary component of vanilla).

Molecular Formula: C₁₈H₂₇NO₃ Molecular Weight: 305.41 g/mol IUPAC Name: (E)-N-(4-hydroxy-3-methoxybenzyl)-8-methylnon-6-enamide

How Does it Cause the "Burn"?

Capsaicin binds to the TRPV1 receptor — a protein in the cell membranes of pain-sensing neurons. This receptor normally responds to actual heat (above 43°C), but capsaicin tricks it into firing at room temperature.

Capsaicin doesn't actually raise your body temperature — it just convinces your nervous system that it does. The "heat" is entirely a neurological illusion.

The Scoville Scale

The concentration of capsaicin in a pepper is measured using the Scoville Heat Unit (SHU) scale. Pure capsaicin measures between 15–16 million SHU. A jalapeño sits at around 2,500–8,000 SHU by comparison.

Why Do Plants Make It?

Capsaicin is an evolutionary defence mechanism — it deters mammals (who would crush the seeds) while leaving birds unaffected (who lack the TRPV1 receptor sensitivity and help spread seeds).
